Optimizing Multi-Head Attention Chains

The core operation of transformer-based AI is the attention mechanism, specifically the chain of matrix multiplications: (Q × KT) × V. For specific sequence lengths and head dimensions, the order of these multiplications can change the compute cost by over 60%.

The Problem

Matrix Multiplication Is Associative, But Not Equally Costly

In linear algebra, matrix multiplication is associative: (A × B) × C = A × (B × C). However, in computational mathematics, the choice between these two forms dictates the size of the intermediate matrix and the total number of Floating Point Operations (FLOPs).

For Multi-Head Attention (MHA), we deal with shapes defined by SeqLen (sequence length) and HeadDim (dimension per head). When sequence lengths grow large, the intermediate "Attention Matrix" (Q × KT) becomes a memory and compute bottleneck.

Symbolic Insight

Shape-Aware Re-association

Consider a standard attention block where:
Q = [128, 64] (SeqLen, HeadDim)
K = [256, 64] (ContextLen, HeadDim)
V = [256, 64] (ContextLen, HeadDim)

  • Naive Form: (Q × KT) × V results in an intermediate matrix of [128, 256].
  • Optimized Form: Q × (KT × V) results in an intermediate matrix of only [64, 64].

For these specific dimensions, the optimized form reduces total operations from 4.2M FLOPs to 1.5M FLOPs—a saving of ~64%.

Why It Matters

Beyond FLOPs: Memory Bandwidth and Throughput

While FLOP reduction is the most visible benefit, the optimized form also significantly reduces the memory bandwidth requirements. By avoiding the materialization of the large intermediate attention matrix, the system keeps more of the computation within the GPU's fast L2 cache.

This optimization is particularly relevant for Cross-Attention (where context length often exceeds head dimension) and Flash-Attention style implementations where efficient memory access is the primary goal.
