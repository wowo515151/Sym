Gamma Function Reflection Formula

The Gamma function Γ(z) is a fundamental extension of the factorial to complex numbers. One of its most elegant properties is the reflection formula, which links the Gamma function to trigonometry: Γ(z)Γ(1-z) = π / sin(πz).

The Problem

Symmetry Across the Half-Integer

For many calculations in physics and statistics, we encounter products of Gamma functions with "reflected" arguments, such as Γ(1/4) and Γ(3/4). Calculating these directly via integrals is often impossible.

The reflection formula provides a bridge. It allows us to "reflect" a value across the z = 1/2 line, converting a product of two complex Gamma functions into a single trigonometric evaluation.

Symbolic Insight

The Factorial Connection

Γ(z + 1) = z × Γ(z)

When combined with Euler's limit definition, a symbolic engine can derive that the poles of Γ(z) at non-positive integers perfectly cancel the zeros of the sine function when multiplied in this specific reflected form.

  • Identity: π / sin(πz)
Why It Matters

Quantum Mechanics and Residue Calculus

This identity is a cornerstone of residue calculus and is used to solve complex integrals in quantum mechanics (specifically in the derivation of the Beta function and scattering amplitudes).

In numerical libraries, the reflection formula is used to compute Gamma for negative arguments, where the standard Stirling approximation fails due to the function's highly oscillatory behavior and poles.
